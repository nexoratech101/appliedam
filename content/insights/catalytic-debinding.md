Metal Fused Filament Fabrication (Metal FFF) is an additive manufacturing process used to produce complex metal parts using metal-bound composite filaments. This technique is particularly valuable when fast delivery times are crucial or when traditional manufacturing methods face limitations.

![3D Printed Metal Parts](/images/insights/catalytic-debinding/image-1.jpeg)
*3D printed metal parts produced via Metal FFF*

The process consists of three key stages: printing, debinding, and sintering. Industrial 3D printers such as [UltiMaker](https://www.linkedin.com/company/ultimaker/), [Raise3D Technologies](https://www.linkedin.com/company/raise3d-inc./), and [Markforged](https://www.linkedin.com/company/markforged/) are widely used for Metal FFF. One key advantage of this technique is that no special skills are required for the printing stage. However, the secondary steps, debinding and sintering, are critical for achieving the best results.

In Metal FFF, the printed part is called the "green part", which consists of metal particles bound together by a polymer. During debinding, the polymer binder is removed, transforming the part into a porous and fragile structure known as the "brown part". Finally, during sintering, the metal particles fuse together, resulting in a fully dense finished metal component.

![Process of the Metal FFF](/images/insights/catalytic-debinding/image-2.png)

This article focuses on the critical factors influencing filament properties and the catalytic debinding process, one of the three main debinding methods, which plays a key role in determining the final part's quality and performance.

The three main methods of debinding are solvent, thermal, and catalytic.

## Catalytic Debinding

Catalytic debinding involves the rapid decomposition of binders into volatile molecules in a catalyst atmosphere. It is the fastest of the three methods and has become an industry standard. [Forward AM](https://www.linkedin.com/company/forwardam/) developed and introduced this technique to the Metal FFF market, and companies like [XERION BERLIN LABORATORIES](https://www.linkedin.com/company/xerion-berlin-laboratories-gmbh/) have adopted it widely.

Several factors affect the successful removal of binders, with material composition being one of the most critical.

## Composition of Metal Filament

Metal filaments are composed of 70-80% metal powder with the remainder being a polymer binder system. Catamold binder systems typically have two or more polymers, each serving a different function at different stages of the process:

- **Main binder** - makes up 50-90% of the polymer content and is removed during the debinding stage. Low molecular weight materials are used to increase viscosity and minimise interference between larger particles.
- **Backbone polymer** - constitutes up to 50% of the polymer and is not removed until the sintering stage. It holds the metal particles together and is typically a thermoplastic.
- **Additives** - make up to 10% of the binder and serve to stabilise and disperse the binders through the metal particles.

## Importance of Particle Distribution

The heterogeneous distribution of particles within the filament can have a significant impact on the debinding process. Poor distribution can result in instability and incomplete binder removal in the brown part. In the sintered part, this can manifest as surface cracks, internal pores, uneven shrinkage, and warping.

The material composition and uniformity of properties throughout the filament directly affect the debinding process. When selecting a metal filament supplier, it is crucial to choose an experienced and reputable manufacturer to mitigate these potential challenges.
